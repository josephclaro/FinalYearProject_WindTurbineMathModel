This project addresses calculating the lift generated by an inviscid fluid flow with constant velocity on an airfoil, and the relevance of results in the context of a wind turbine.

The problem is at first approached in two dimensions, dealing with the cross-section of a typical airfoil. Mathematically the geometry of an airfoil isn’t easily defined, so initial work concerns a circular profile. The Joukowsky transform - a complex plane conformal mapping - is then applied to the circle and the surrounding fluid. With the right parameters this transform can recreate airfoil-like geometries. Fluid flow adjustments are then made to remove unphysical properties.

Results are then extended to three dimensions, taking blade rotation and relative wind speed into consideration. The work builds on classical airfoil theory techniques, as covered in textbooks by M.T Milne-Thomson, D.J Acheson and John D. Anderson Jr.

Calculations neglect boundary layer separation and the resulting turbulent airflow, and assumes sensor data is used to optimally align the turbine’s blades with the wind. Relevant visuals, including those of the Joukowsky transform in action, are created in bothDesmos and MATLAB. The latter is also used for numerical approximations where appropriate.
